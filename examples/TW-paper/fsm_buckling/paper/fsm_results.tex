\section{Verification}\label{sec:results}
The FSM is verified against three-dimensional shell eigenvalue analysis with the same MITC4 flat-facet
Reissner--Mindlin element used throughout OpenSG, on axially-compressed cylinders, a square tube and a
tapered cone. The walls are (i) isotropic ($E=\SI{200}{\giga\pascal}$, $\nu=0.3$) and (ii) a
balanced-symmetric $[\pm45^\circ]_s$ carbon/epoxy laminate ($E_1=\SI{140}{\giga\pascal}$,
$E_2=\SI{10}{\giga\pascal}$, $G_{12}=\SI{5}{\giga\pascal}$, $\nu_{12}=0.3$), thickness $t=\SI{0.02}{\metre}$.
The pre-buckling state is uniform axial (meridional) compression; the FSM and the shell FEA are driven by the
\emph{same} membrane resultant $\NN$, so the comparison isolates the buckling formulation.

The flat-facet shell element carries no stiffness about its normal (the drilling rotation), so a small
stabilization spring is required. A na\"ive spring about the global $z$-axis is orientation-dependent and
silently softens curved-shell buckling; a spring about the true element normal
$k_{\mathrm{dr}}\,\mathbf n\mathbf n^{\!\top}$ (reducing to the $\theta_z$ term when $\mathbf n=\hat{\mathbf z}$)
is correct on smooth shells but, at a sharp fold, one wall's drilling axis coincides with the neighbour's
\emph{physical} bending rotation, over-stiffening the junction. We therefore apply the spring only at
near-coplanar nodes and let the fold geometry desingularize corners; the value is verified insensitive to
$k_{\mathrm{dr}}$ over three decades. With this stabilization the shell FEA reproduces the classical isotropic
cylinder to $0.999$ and the analytic square-plate $k{=}4$ to $0.99$, and is used as the reference below.

\subsection{Isotropic cylinder}
For $R=\SI{1}{\metre}$, $L=\SI{2}{\metre}$ ($R/t=50$) the classical critical membrane force is
$N_{\mathrm{cr}}^{\mathrm{cl}}=E t^2/[R\sqrt{3(1-\nu^2)}]=\SI{4.842e7}{\newton\per\metre}$. The
three-dimensional shell FEA gives $\SI{4.838e7}{\newton\per\metre}$ ($0.999\,N_{\mathrm{cr}}^{\mathrm{cl}}$),
converging to the classical value from above under mesh refinement. The multi-harmonic FSM returns
$\SI{4.685e7}{\newton\per\metre}$, i.e.\ $0.968$ of the shell FEA and $0.967$ of the classical value: a
formulation floor of about $3\%$ between the analytic Kirchhoff strip and the shear-flexible facet shell,
independent of the number of harmonics (the isotropic wall has no $16/26$ coupling to recover). The FSM
costs $\SI{4.5}{\second}$ against several minutes for the $\num{174240}$-DOF ($n_c=240$) shell eigenproblem.

\subsection{Anisotropic $[\pm45^\circ]_s$ cylinder: the multi-harmonic recovery}
A single harmonic returns $\SI{8.73e6}{\newton\per\metre}$, $1.49\times$ the shell FEA
($\SI{5.87e6}{\newton\per\metre}$): the near-$50\%$ over-prediction is the bend--twist coupling
$D_{16},D_{26}$ annihilated by \eqref{eq:orth}. Restoring it through the multi-harmonic series
\eqref{eq:series}--\eqref{eq:kcouple} converges the load downward as opposite-parity harmonics couple
(Table~\ref{tab:multi}): from $1.49\times$ at $M{=}1$ to $\mathbf{1.014}\times$ by $M{\ge}12$---within
$1.4\%$ of the three-dimensional shell buckling. The residual is the transverse-shear flexibility of the
$\pm45$ wall, which the present Kirchhoff strip omits; it is small for this thin tube but a
Reissner--Mindlin strip (future work) would close it, and it is essential for foam-core sandwich panels.
The isotropic ratio is insensitive to $M$ ($0.968$ throughout), confirming that the $M$-convergence for
$[\pm45^\circ]$ is the anisotropic coupling and not a discretization artifact.

The first two buckling mode shapes are compared between the direct shell FEA and the FSM-RM reconstruction
(cross-section eigenvector times the longitudinal sinusoid). The isotropic cylinder is
\emph{Koiter-degenerate}---a whole family of axial/circumferential wavenumber pairs $(m,n)$ buckles at

therefore realize different members of this critical family. Fig.~\ref{fig:modeiso} shows, for each FEA

bend--twist coupling $D_{16}$ selects a \emph{unique} helical buckle---which the multi-harmonic FSM
reproduces mode for mode (Fig.~\ref{fig:modem45}).

\subsection{Square tube: box-corner coupling and the per-station regime}\label{sec:square}
The square tube ($a\times a$, $a=\SI{1}{\metre}$, $t=\SI{0.02}{\metre}$, $L=\SI{2}{\metre}$) tests the
FSM closed-section assembly at $90^\circ$ folds. For an equal-walled box the lowest local mode is the
alternating-wall mode, whose corners rotate freely, so each wall behaves as a simply-supported plate with
buckling coefficient $k=4$~\citep{timoshenko1961}; the analytic value is $N_{\mathrm{cr}}=4\pi^2 D/a^2$,
i.e.\ $P_{\mathrm{cr}}=\SI{2.314e7}{\newton\per\metre}$. The FSM returns $\SI{2.311e7}{}$ (within $0.1\%$):
the shared rotation $\theta$ (about the longitudinal fold axis, Eq.~\ref{eq:transform}) enforces the corner
compatibility and mutual restraint exactly, so the box coupling is captured with no drilling degree of
freedom. The drilling-stabilized shell FEA gives $\SI{2.288e7}{}$ ($0.99$ of analytic, $k=4$), and
FSM/FEA${}=1.010$.

For the \emph{tapered} square ($a:\,1.0\!\to\!0.5$ over $L=2$) the applied membrane grows as $1/a$ while the
plate capacity grows as $1/a^2$, so $P_{\mathrm{cr}}\!\propto\!1/a$ and the \emph{root} (widest wall)
governs---opposite to the cone. Here the per-station FSM is conservative: FSM/FEA${}=0.82$ (iso) and $0.93$
($[\pm45^\circ]$), Table~\ref{tab:sqcone}. The reason is the regime of \eqref{eq:min}: the square plate-buckle
half-wavelength is comparable to the wall width $a\!\sim\!\SI{1}{\metre}$, itself comparable to the taper
length, so the wall narrows appreciably \emph{within a single buckle} and stiffens the true mode above the
prismatic-root estimate. This is the expected lower-bound behaviour of the per-station representation when the
buckle is not short compared with the taper; it is safe (conservative) for design.

\subsection{Tapered cone: connected and different cross-sections}\label{sec:cone}
The tapered cone ($R_1=\SI{1}{\metre}\!\to\!R_2=\SI{0.5}{\metre}$ over $L=\SI{2}{\metre}$,
$\cos\alpha=0.970$) is the complementary regime: the shell buckle half-wavelength $\sqrt{Rt}\!\approx\!0.14$
is much \emph{shorter} than the taper, so each station buckles quasi-prismatically. The \JAXFEA\ buckles the
full three-dimensional shell cone; the \RMFSM\ pathway represents the cone by three cross-sections
(root, mid, tip) and takes the minimum FSM factor \eqref{eq:min}. Both are driven by the analytic meridional
pre-stress $N(x)=-P/[2\pi R(x)\cos\alpha]$.

For the cone the FSM factor is smallest at the \emph{tip} ($R_2=\SI{0.5}{\metre}$, highest $N\!\propto\!1/R$),
so \eqref{eq:min} localizes the critical cross-section; the isotropic case matches the full three-dimensional
cone to $0.5\%$ ($0.995$) and the $[\pm45^\circ]$ case to $3.9\%$ ($1.039$)---the same transverse-shear-limited
margin as the prismatic cylinder. The first two eigenvalues are the near-degenerate cosine/sine circumferential
pair. Together the cone and square bracket the method: the per-station FSM is essentially exact when the buckle
is local (shell-type, short wavelength) and a safe lower bound when it spans the taper (plate-type), introducing
no error beyond the constitutive one in the former regime and validating the cross-section pathway for
non-prismatic (blade) geometries.
