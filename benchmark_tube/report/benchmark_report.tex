\begin{abstract}
We benchmark a thin-walled Mechanics-of-Structure-Genome (MSG) shell
cross-sectional analysis---in both Reissner--Mindlin (RM, $C^0$) and Kirchhoff
($C^1$) wall kinematics---against a three-dimensional (3D) solid finite-element
cross-sectional analysis (FEniCSx), the latter taking the role usually played by
VABS. Two circular composite tubes are studied: an isotropic tube and a balanced
anisotropic $[45/\!-\!45]$ tube. The wall slenderness $\hR$ is swept over five
values from thin-shell ($0.01$) to thick-shell ($0.20$), and the shell wall is
referenced at either the mid-surface (``centre'') or the outer mould line
(``OML''). The full Timoshenko $6\times6$ stiffness is compared term by term and
the isotropic case is additionally checked against the exact annulus closed form.
\emph{Key results.} At the centre reference, the shell reproduces the solid to
$<1\%$ on every non-zero stiffness ($EA$, $GA$, $GJ$, $EI$) across the entire
thin-to-thick range, for both RM and Kirchhoff. At the OML reference the error
grows roughly linearly with $\hR$: $EA$ and $EI$ over-predict by $\approx h/2R$
(the $(1+z/R)$ curvature/arc-length factor) and the two shell models diverge on
$GA$ and $GJ$. The $[45/\!-\!45]$ tube exhibits the expected high torsional
stiffness ($GJ/EA\approx0.85$), captured to $<1\%$ at the centre; its
extension--twist coupling is near zero (balanced layup). The practical
recommendation is to reference the wall at its mid-surface, where the thin-walled
shell model is essentially exact through $\hR=0.20$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cross-sectional homogenization reduces a slender composite structure to a 1D beam
with an effective $6\times6$ stiffness, after which a global beam solution and a
dehomogenization (3D stress recovery) complete the analysis. For thin-walled
sections, a \emph{shell} idealization of the wall is far cheaper than a 2D
\emph{solid} discretization, but its accuracy depends on the wall slenderness
$\hR$ and on the choice of wall reference surface. This report quantifies both
dependencies by benchmarking an MSG thin-walled shell model (RM and Kirchhoff)
against a 3D solid analysis for isotropic and $[45/\!-\!45]$ tubes, over a
thin-to-thick $\hR$ sweep, at the mid-surface and OML references.

%================================================================
\section{Model and method}
\subsection{Thin-walled MSG shell (summary)}
The wall is a 1D structure genome (the mid-surface curve) whose through-thickness
behaviour is condensed into an $8\times8$ plate law
\begin{equation}
\begin{Bmatrix}\bm N\\ \bm M\\ \bm Q\end{Bmatrix}=
\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf A&\mathbf B&\bm0\\ \mathbf B&\mathbf D&\bm0\\
\bm0&\bm0&\bG\end{bmatrix}
\begin{Bmatrix}\bm\varepsilon^0\\ \bm\kappa\\ \bm\gamma^s\end{Bmatrix},
\end{equation}
with the membrane--bending block $[\mathbf A,\mathbf B;\mathbf B,\mathbf D]$ from
classical lamination theory and the transverse-shear block $\bG$ from a
first-order shear-deformation (FSDT) 3D-equilibrium argument (reducing to
$\tfrac56 Gh$ for an isotropic plate); this is an engineering approximation to
the asymptotically-exact transverse-shear stiffness, which in the rigorous RM
shell theory follows from energy minimization rather than an assumed correction
factor~\cite{yu2002rmshell}. Two wall kinematics are used on the same
genome: a Kirchhoff model (no transverse shear, $C^1$ cubic Hermite) and a
Reissner--Mindlin model (transverse shear retained, $C^0$ Lagrange with selective
reduced integration for the shear energy). The Euler--Bernoulli ($4\times4$) and
Timoshenko ($6\times6$) stiffnesses follow from the asymptotic fluctuation fields
$\mathbf V_0$ and $\mathbf V_1$. Full kinematics and the strain field are given in
the companion paper; here we focus on the benchmark.

\subsection{Reference surface}
The shell ABD is referenced either to the wall mid-surface (\emph{centre},
$\mathrm{frac}=0.5$) or to the outer mould line (\emph{OML}, $\mathrm{frac}=0$).
A reference shift by a through-thickness offset $z_0$ uses the parallel-axis
relation $\mathbf B\!\to\!\mathbf B-z_0\mathbf A$,
$\mathbf D\!\to\!\mathbf D-2z_0\mathbf B+z_0^2\mathbf A$, and the wall reference
curvature is $k_{22}=-1/r_{\mathrm{ref}}$ with $r_{\mathrm{ref}}=R$ (centre) or
$R+h/2$ (OML). The present flat-plate ABD omits the $(1+z/R)$ shell metric in the
through-thickness integration; the consequence is quantified below. For the
symmetric tubes studied here the tension and shear centres coincide with the
geometric centre, so the centre-referenced $6\times6$ is itself the
reference-invariant comparison~\cite{yu2012vabs}; the OML results are a deliberate
reference-sensitivity study, not an alternative ``stiffness'' of the tube.

\subsection{Solid reference}
The 3D reference is a 2D solid cross-sectional analysis (FEniCSx, first-order
quadrilaterals) with the full 3D constitutive law and the lamina orientation in
the element frame; it plays the role of VABS. The annulus is meshed with
$N_c=200$ circumferential and $N_r=16$ radial elements (8 per ply for
$[45/\!-\!45]$). For the isotropic tube the solid reproduces the exact annulus
closed form ($EA=E\pi(r_o^2-r_i^2)$, $EI=E\tfrac\pi4(r_o^4-r_i^4)$, $GJ=2EI\,G/E$)
to $<0.1\%$ at every $\hR$, confirming mesh convergence.

\section{Results}
The per-stiffness tables (errors relative to the solid) are collected in
Tables~\ref{tab:iso_EA}--\ref{tab:aniso_exttwist}; the convergence with $\hR$ is
plotted in Figs.~\ref{fig:err_iso}--\ref{fig:abs_aniso}.

\subsection{Isotropic tube}
At the \textbf{centre} reference the shell is essentially exact:
$EA$ within $0.01\%$, and $GA$, $GJ$, $EI$ within $1\%$ even at $\hR=0.20$
(Table~\ref{tab:iso_EA}--\ref{tab:iso_EI}, Fig.~\ref{fig:err_iso}). Both RM and
Kirchhoff overlap with the solid and with the analytic annulus values.
At the \textbf{OML} reference the error grows almost linearly in $\hR$:
$EA$ and $EI$ over-predict by $\approx h/2R$ ($+10\%$ and $+9.4\%$ at
$\hR=0.20$), consistent with the OML radius being $(1+h/2R)$ times the
mid-surface radius (the omitted curvature metric). $GA$ under-predicts and the
two models split ($GJ$: RM $+20\%$, KF $-10\%$ at $\hR=0.20$).

\subsection{Anisotropic $[45/\!-\!45]$ tube}
The balanced $[45/\!-\!45]$ layup yields a high torsional stiffness
($GJ/EA\approx0.85$), reproduced to $<1\%$ at the centre reference across all
$\hR$ (Table~\ref{tab:aniso_GJ}, Fig.~\ref{fig:err_aniso}). $EA$, $GA$ and $EI$
behave as in the isotropic case: $<1\%$ at the centre, $\sim h/2R$ drift at the
OML. The extension--twist coupling $C_{14}$ is two-to-three orders below $EA$
(the $+45$ and $-45$ contributions nearly cancel); the large \emph{relative}
error there (Table~\ref{tab:aniso_exttwist}) is therefore on a physically
negligible term and is not indicative of a modelling deficiency. (A single
$-45^\circ$ ply, by contrast, has a significant tension--twist coupling.)

\subsection{Reference surface: centre vs OML}
The decisive finding is the reference dependence. The mid-surface reference
keeps every principal stiffness within $1\%$ of the solid from thin to thick
shells; the OML reference incurs an $O(\hR)$ error that reaches $10$--$20\%$ at
$\hR=0.20$. The over-prediction of $EA$/$EI$ matches the $(1+h/2R)$ arc-length
factor expected when the wall is integrated about the outer surface without the
$(1+z/R)$ shell metric. For design use the wall should be referenced at its
mid-surface.

\subsection{Reissner--Mindlin vs Kirchhoff}
At the centre reference RM and Kirchhoff are both within $1\%$ and nearly
coincide; the transverse-shear kinematics of RM matter most for $GA$ and for the
OML reference, where the two models diverge (RM and KF bracket the solid $GJ$).
For the recovered transverse-shear \emph{stress} (not a homogenization quantity)
the RM model is essential, since Kirchhoff returns it as identically zero; that
behaviour is documented in the companion paper.

\section{Discussion}
Three points stand out. (i) The thin-walled MSG shell is not merely a thin-limit
approximation: at the mid-surface reference it reproduces the 3D solid to $<1\%$
well into the thick-shell range ($\hR=0.20$), for isotropic and balanced
anisotropic walls alike, in both RM and Kirchhoff forms. (ii) The dominant
error source for thick walls is the \emph{reference-surface} choice, not the
shell kinematics: the OML reference omits the $(1+z/R)$ curvature metric and
incurs an $O(\hR)$ bias, whereas the mid-surface reference is symmetric and the
leading curvature term integrates to zero~\cite{yu2002rmshell}. (iii) RM and
Kirchhoff agree at the centre; the model split that appears \emph{only} at the
OML reflects the off-centric configuration activating a transverse-shear measure
($2\gamma_{12}\!\sim\!\kappa_1 h$) that the Kirchhoff kinematics cannot
carry~\cite{bagla2025}, which is why $GA$ and $GJ$---the shear-sensitive
terms---are where the two models diverge. RM's standing advantage is in
transverse-shear quantities ($GA$ and, in dehomogenization, the transverse-shear
stress, which Kirchhoff returns as identically zero). The sub-$1\%$
centre-reference residual on $GA$/$GJ$ is this Kirchhoff-vs-RM shear gap (and the
FSDT shear approximation), not a missing shear-correction factor. The small
extension--twist coupling of the balanced $[45/\!-\!45]$ tube ($A_{16}=A_{26}=0$,
the $\pm45$ contributions cancelling~\cite{bagla2025}) is a reminder to judge
couplings by absolute magnitude, not relative error; a single $-45^\circ$ ply,
by contrast, carries an $O(1)$ tension--twist coupling.

\medskip\noindent The physical interpretations above were cross-checked against
the asymptotic shell/beam literature (the curvature Jacobian and $O(\hR)$
energy ordering~\cite{yu2002rmshell}; the off-centric transverse-shear
activation and balanced-layup decoupling~\cite{bagla2025}; the reference-centre
invariants~\cite{yu2012vabs}).

\section{Conclusions}
A thin-walled MSG shell cross-sectional analysis, referenced at the wall
mid-surface, matches a 3D solid (VABS-equivalent) analysis to better than $1\%$
on the full Timoshenko stiffness of isotropic and $[45/\!-\!45]$ circular tubes,
across $\hR=0.01$--$0.20$, in both Reissner--Mindlin and Kirchhoff forms. The
outer-mould-line reference introduces an $O(\hR)$ curvature/arc-length error that
reaches $10$--$20\%$ for thick walls and splits the two shell models; the
mid-surface reference is therefore recommended. The high torsional stiffness of
the balanced anisotropic layup is captured to $<1\%$. These results delineate the
validity envelope of the thin-walled shell model and confirm it is accurate far
beyond the nominally ``thin'' regime when the wall reference is chosen correctly.
